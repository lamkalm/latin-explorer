Virgil's Legacy

Virgil's poetry didn't end with ancient Rome — it shaped the modern world. Discover how his words appear in seals, monuments, and institutions that define our civilization.

Publius Vergilius Maro (70–19 BCE)
Rome's greatest poet, author of the Aeneid
Great Seal of the United States
National Symbol • 1782
"Novus Ordo Seclorum"
"A New Order of the Ages"
Modern Context
Appears on the reverse side of every U.S. dollar bill. Virgil wrote these words in his Fourth Eclogue, prophesying a new golden age. American founders adopted it to symbolize the birth of a new nation.

Annuit Coeptis
Great Seal Motto • From Aeneid Book 9
"Annuit Coeptis"
"He [God] Has Favored Our Undertakings"
Modern Context
The obverse motto of the Great Seal, above the pyramid. Taken from Virgil's Aeneid where Jupiter reassures Venus that he favors the Trojans' mission to found Rome.

West Point Motto
United States Military Academy
"Duty, Honor, Country"
Though not directly from Virgil, this embodies the heroic ideals he celebrated in the Aeneid — duty to mission, honor in battle, service to nation.
Virgil Connection
While not Virgil's words, the motto reflects the same Roman values Virgil immortalized. Aeneas's journey was one of duty and honor, founding a nation through sacrifice.

European Royal Mottos
Monarchies & Institutions
"Dieu et Mon Droit"
"God and My Right" — Motto of the British Monarch (from Norman French, but in Virgil's tradition of heroic kingship)
Virgil's Influence
Virgil's portrayal of Aeneas as the ideal king — pious, brave, and divinely favored — influenced European concepts of monarchy and leadership for centuries.
